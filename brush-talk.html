Sinitic Brushtalk:
Using Classical and Literary Chinese as a Written Lingua Franca
in Premodern East Asia

People in East Asia – today’s China, North and South Korea, Japan and Vietnam – speak very different languages.

Have you ever wondered…

  • For over a thousand years till 1900s, how did people in premodern East Asia communicate when crossing borders for transactional purposes, e.g., checking identities, asking for directions, buying and selling, or ordering food?
  • What about interactional communication such as expressing opinions, debating controversial issues, asserting (dis)agreements, or even showcasing literary talents for the sheer pleasure of artistic appreciation?

Some highlights

  • For over 1000 years, Classical and Literary Chinese functioned as a written lingua franca in cross-border communication in East Asia.
  • Sinitic brushtalk between Chinese, Korean, Japanese and Vietnamese literati was very common.
  • The main purpose of Sinitic brushtalk may be transactional or interactional.
  • Sinitic brushtalk allows for inter-ethnic, but also intra-ethnic communication
    e.g. brushtalk between Wang Guohua王國華 and Jao Tsung-I饒宗頤:
    「我與饒公交流的最大障礙是語言。我是山東口音，饒公是潮州口音。年齡上又是兩代人，百歲饒公是國學大師，語言與學問上的落差，使我們交流時經常借助於筆談。一次交流談話，一般要寫滿一個小本子。這也留下了許多珍貴資料。」
    Cited from G. Wang (2015) 《心經簡林》 (Xīnjīng Jiănlín, ‘Wisdom Path’), Chung Hwa Book Company, p. 50.
  • As a written lingua franca and language contact phenomenon, Sinitic brushtalk is clearly under-researched linguistically and sociolinguistically.

What is brushtalk (also ‘brush conversation’)?

Eight discrete usages of the term 筆談 (Y. Wang 王勇 & Xie 謝詠 2015)

  1. creative genre expressing personal thoughts or reflections
  2. title of Hong Kong-based magazine in 1941
  3. synonym of literary genre of epistolary writing
  4. brush-and-ink meaning-making practice preferred to speech (guard against possible information leakage to eavesdroppers, especially life-and-death circumstance, political intrigue or espionage)
  5. only means of communication (e.g. with bed-ridden patients)
  6. vernacular-driven writing style (Zhou Zuoren周作人, 1920s)
  7. mode of communication between Chinese dialect speakers
  8. mode of cross-border, intercultural communication involving speakers from different countries with no shared spoken language

How does it work in the modern world?

Brushtalk in modern setting: Sinitic brushtalk as a sui generis lingua-cultural tradition in Sinographic East Asia

我與饒公交流的最大障礙是語言。我是山東口音，饒公是潮州口音。年齡上又是兩代人，百歲饒公是國學大師，語言與學問上的落差，使我們交流時經常借助於筆談。一次交流談話，一般要寫滿一個小本子。這也留下了許多珍貴資料。
from G. Wang (2015: 50)
‘In my verbal exchange with Master Jao Tsung-I, language is our greatest communication barrier. My speech is coloured with Shandong accent, while Master Jao’s is Chaozhou-accented. Age difference is another factor, as Master Jao is older than me by one generation. Master Jao being a centenarian with erudite knowledge in Sinology, plus the huge gap in our vernaculars and scholarly insights, we often rely on brushtalk in our interaction. A booklet full of scribbles would often result after our in-depth ‘conversational’ exchange. What is good about it is that plenty of precious materials are preserved for future reference.’ (our translation)

Example of trilateral brushtalk: Vietnamese-Chinese-Japanese

Between Vietnamese anticolonial leader Phan Bội Châu 潘佩珠, his Chinese contacts – reformist Liang Qichao 梁啓超 and revolutionary leader Sun Yat-sen孫逸仙, and Japanese leaders in 1905–1906

“Here [in Japan] Phan Boi Chau sought out Liang Qichao, a refugee from the wrath of the Emperor Dowager, and has several extended discussions with him. Their common language was Chinese, but in written form, for while Phan Boi Chau was able to read and write Chinese his Sino-Vietnamese pronunciation was unintelligible to his interlocutor. They sat together at a table and passed back and forth to each other sheets covered with Chinese characters written with a brush.”
(DeFrancis 1977: 161)

Role of brushtalk in opening of Tokugawa Japan

“I found myself quite an object of interest; and as they set a great value on Chinese characters and compositions, whenever I went to the hall of reception many of them were sure to ask me to write on fans for them. The fans I inscribed during a month while we were at Yokohama could not be fewer than five hundred. The applications were, indeed, troublesome, and the writing took up much of my time, but it was difficult to decline acceding to their pressing requests.”
(Luo 1854: 401; cited in Tao 2005: 104)

What is the theoretical significance of Sinitic brushtalk?

Classical or literary Chinese was used as a written lingua franca in premodern East Asia – an age-old lingua-cultural practice

“The welcoming of the visitors from Parhae 渤海 began with an exchange of Chinese poems between Sugawara no Michizane 菅原道真 and the other Japanese hosts and the dignitaries from Parhae. Only when Chinese poetry had been exchanged could formal negotiations begin. Depending on the availability of interpreters, it can easily be imagined that both parties might be compelled to depend on the writing brush as the sole means of conducting negotiations.” (Keaveney 2009:7)

Is the function of Chinese as a written lingua franca in East Asia unique or sui generis?

To our knowledge, no such function has been documented in other historically comparable regional lingua francas such as Latin or Arabic. Part of the explanation for the sui generis written lingua franca function of classical or literary Chinese may be found in the morpho-syllabic, non-alphabetic writing system.

Theoretical significance of Sinitic brushtalk in language contact research

Learning difficulty? Two main linguistic differences

  • Chinese – classical or modern, spoken or written – typologically an isolating language with verb-medial word order (i.e., SVO) and makes minimal use of morphology
  • Japanese, like Korean: agglutinative, with verb-final SOV word order, and marks syntactic functions obligatorily with particles
  • Sinograms being non-alphabetic, relationship between spelling and pronunciation oblique.
  • All this adds up to “a seemingly insurmountable linguistic barrier” for Japanese students (Mizumura 2015: 107), conceivably for Korean students as well.

Acquisitional

  • Some evidence of grammatical functions of brushtalkers’ L1 mapped onto L2 (Classical Chinese) grammatical markers
  • interrogative morpheme 何 (pīnyīn hé; Jap. なにnani; Kor: ha; Vietnamese: hà/sao/nào/gì; see examples (1) and (2)) and
  • sentence-final question marker 耶 (pinyin yé; Jap. かka; Kor. ya; see examples (2), (4), (6))
  • How is mapping done – is it always consistent ?
  • Brushtalk research: contributes to East Asian historical linguistics, sociolinguistics, and contrastive language contact research

Typological

  • Plenty of brush talk evidence shows that Classical Chinese became a productive and lively communicative resource with tremendous artistic meaning-making potential in the hands of creative literati, especially when poetic elements are involved in their spontaneous artistic improvisations. How is that possible?
  • The borrowing constraints operate in such a way that (Field 2002):
  • language with fusional morphology can borrow grammatical morphemes from agglutinating or isolating language;
  • agglutinating language can borrow grammatical morphemes from isolating or agglutinating language, but not from fusional language
  • By contrast, an isolating language cannot borrow morphemes from a fusional or agglutinating language.
  • Field’s (2002) postulated hierarchies of borrowability is clearly supported by our Sinitic brush talk data.
  • For over a thousand years until the dawn of the modern era, there is ample evidence of Sinitic borrowing, principally in the form of graphic borrowing (Hansell 2002), to the point of spawning indigenized sinograms (e.g., Jap. wasei-kango 和製漢語; Viet. chữ nôm, the latter being written as 字喃 or 𡨸喃) that were unintelligible to Chinese literati in China.
  • This shows clearly that agglutinative languages like Japanese and Korean can borrow from an isolated language like Chinese.
  • But what about the borrowability of written Chinese into Vietnamese, another isolating language?

Are there any relevant studies?

Research in English

  • Little research on Sinitic brushtalk as language contact phenomenon (e.g. written lingua franca) in premodern East Asia
  • Keaveney (2009: 2–9): brief historical overview of brushtalk as deep-rooted lingua-cultural practice in East Asia
  • Tao (2005) examines the role of brushtalk in early Japanese-U.S. diplomacy in mid-1850s
  • Howland (1996) shows how brushtalk in Classical Chinese enabled Chinese and Japanese scholars to display Sinitic nexus of civilizations while projecting distinctive lingua-cultural representations
  • Clements (2018) and Denecke (2015) both focus on functions, but from different angles
  • Hur and Cho (2016) examine diplomatic relations between Japan and Korea during that period.

Denecke (2014: 209)

The greatest advantage of the Chinese script (...) is that it enabled literate people in premodern East Asia to communicate directly in the absence of a common spoken language. Chinese-style writing was the East Asian lingua franca, or we should rather say scripta franca, because unlike elites who wrote and conversed in Latin in medieval and early modern Europe, Chinese-style writing was written language, a grapholect.”

Research in Chinese

  • Wang & Xie (2015): collection of conference papers on Chinese-Japanese and Chinese-Korean brushtalk produced by literati, gentry and diplomats, but also by Japanese monks and seafarers in different epochs
  • Yuzhen Liu (2010): brushtalk data collected from Qing diplomats while they discharged duties as Chinese ambassadors or embassy personnel in Meiji Japan
  • Y. Wang (2013): adduces sociocultural significance of brushtalk as shown in travelogues by Korean and Vietnamese envoys
  • Yu & Liang (2013) exemplify brush talk between Chinese and Vietnamese officials, which reflect history of international relations between the two countries
  • Zhang (2012) outlines six brush conversations produced by Vietnamese envoy Lê Quý Đôn 黎貴惇, a famous scholar-official dispatched by late Lê dynasty to China in 1761

Research in Japanese

  • Matsuura (2007): examines discussion and implementation of mutual arrangements regarding return of crews shipwrecked in different parts of East Asia – 17th to 19th centuries
  • Matsuura (2009) and (2011): handling of Chinese ships stranded in Korean and Japanese waters, respectively
  • Shipwrecked seafarers and their rescuers: W.-S. Lee (1997); Ogawa (2012); Moon (2018)
  • Japanese official visit to Shanghai on board Senzaimaru 千歲丸 in 1862: Feng (1999); Yokoyama (2002); Fujita (2015, 2016)

Research in Korean

  • Koo (2011) analyzes how knowledge was spread from Korean envoys to Japanese in 17th century by 朝鮮後期通信使筆談唱和集飜譯叢書.
  • Jang (2017) discusses significance of brush conversations between Japanese officials and Korean envoys to Japan in 1764.
  • H.-S. Lee (1996) investigates brush conversations between Korean and Japanese scholars on neo-Confucianism in 1711.
  • Kim et al. (2015) examine exchange on medical knowledge between Korean and Japanese medics through brushtalk.

Research in Vietnamese

  • Lý (2007, 2009) and Trịnh & Đ. T. Nguyễn (2012a, 2012b) study poetic exchange between envoys from Vietnam and Korea.
  • M. T. Nguyễn (2009) and T. T. Nguyễn (2012) examine diplomatic interchange between ambassadors from Vietnam and Korea in China during 18th century.
